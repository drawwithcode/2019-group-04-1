The WhatsApp Revolution

It is estimated that more than 1.5 million people have taken to the streets of Lebanon during the weekend of October 17th. The protests, which have taken place practically in all the cities and towns of the country, and even abroad, have been characterized by their transversal nature: the protesters belong to all sects and social strata, and the object of citizen anger is the entire Government and Lebanese political parties, who are accused of being corrupt and unable to solve the country's problems.

What triggered the protests?

The trigger for the demonstrations was the announcement to impose a tax on telephone calls through voice services over the internet, such as Whatsapp and Telegram. However, the "WhatsApp rate" is only the tip of the iceberg.


Discontent has been brewing over the past eight months, when former Prime Minister Hariri announced "the most austere budget in the history of Lebanon" to reduce one of the highest external debts - € 75.8 billion, which is equivalent to 150% of GDP. Austerity means cuts: first, the rise in an IVA point and then, the reduction of the pensions of officials and milicy.

What do people aim with the protests?

The protests aim to break the current status quo with a government of technocrats. This is the first generation of the post-civil war (1975-1990) that rejects sectarian rules and considered them expired. The confessional political system that rules in Lebanon implies that power should be distributed by quotas; the president must be a Christian; the prime minister, a Sunni Muslim, and the president of Parliament, a Shiite Muslim. For the protesters, this system intoxicates coexistence in the country.


Lebanon is ranked 138th out of 180 on the Transparency International corruption list. The political elite overlaps the economic one: six of the seven Lebanese on the Forbes fortune list in 2019 are politicians: three from the Hariri clan, two from the Miqat clan. One of the demands is that politicians involved in cases of embezzlement of public funds be tried and their property seized.

According to the World Bank, one-third of the population lives below the poverty line. Only 1% of the population accumulates more than a third of the country's wealth, which accentuates social inequality and puts the middle class on the way to extinction.

While the authorities do not define a government that responds to the urgent needs of the Lebanese, the World Bank warned that the number of people in the country living in poverty could increase from one third to half of the population.

What are the consequences of the protests?

Due to the demonstrations, the banks, which control 90% of the currencies, have closed their doors panicking citizens. Moreover, they imposed a limit for withdrawals to avoid a lack of liquidity in dollars. As a consequence, the Lebanese pound (whose parity with the dollar had remained since 1997) has suffered a dramatic depreciation and is changed 45% below. This situation has triggered prices by 20% in just one month.


Because of the decrease of the depreciation, gas stations went on strike due to the impossibility of importing fuel for lack of dollars, and both hospitals and companies warn that they will soon run out of their medicine and food reserves if they do not have access to foreign exchange. In addition, the country's infrastructures, fall apart and the population suffers power cuts of between three and 12 hours a day.

Lebanon now

Lebanon formed a new government with Hassan Diab. He claimed to be working on the new election law and stressed that his cabinet was formed in line with protesters’ expectations. In addition, they claimed that they would work for the return of looted public funds and the independence of the judiciary.


The confirmation of this new government caused a crack between Lebanese people (the ones who trust the new government and the ones who don’t), making protests continue but not as peaceful as they were.
