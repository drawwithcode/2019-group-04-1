“Five requests, not one less”

The conflict in Hong Kong, which began as a peaceful demonstration to protect themselves against a bill to reform the law, ended up becoming a symbol of the defence of democracy and Hong Kong’s Constitution.
Hong Kong is a special administrative region of China. It has its own currency, a political system and their own cultural identity, which is different from those in mainland of China. This relationship of 'independent belonging' is foreseen by the formula "One China, two systems", an expression which indicates the negotiated solution for the return of Hong Kong under Chinese jurisdiction the year 2047.

Today, Hong Kong's legal system still reflects the British model and insists on transparency and fair trial. The principles are guaranteed by the constitution, the Basic Law, which is based on common law and protects rights other than those of the mainland Chinese. Among them are the right to protest, free press and free speech. In general, the law also establishes that the city has "a high degree of autonomy" in all fields except foreign policy and defence. But many residents claim that Beijing is violating these rights in a perceptible way.

What triggered the protests?

The first wave of protests occurred in 2014 when China announced that they wanted to reform the region's electoral system. The protests lasted nearly three months, and it was called "umbrella uprising". The proposal, which was not adopted, was perceived as an extremely restrictive measure of the autonomy of the region since it entailed the equivalent of a "pre-selection" of candidates for leadership in Hong Kong by the Chinese Communist Party (CCP).

The current protests in Hong Kong have part of their origin in the Umbrella Revolution. Both protests have young people as promoters and main protesters, but in the Umbrella Revolution, the Hong Kong people took to the streets to demand universal suffrage and direct election of the local government, while in the current protests they began with a project of law reform.
The mobilizations began the 31st March 2019 as a protest against the proposed reform of the Extradition Law, presented by the Carrie Lam government, that proposed that China could judge Hong Kong criminals.

The extradition law is the practice whereby a state surrenders to another state an individual who is in his territory, but who is subject to criminal prosecution in the other. The amendment would have forced the region to hand over people investigated from Beijing to China for certain crimes. However, the Hongkongers did not agree and understood the risk of human rights violations, and the use of extradition by the Chinese as a pretext to reach the political dissidents who took refuge in Hong Kong.

Street protests officially began on June 9, with a million people taking to the streets. It was a series of peaceful rallies and demonstrations in the streets. Tired of not observing much government reaction, some groups broke into Parliament.

In summer marches became increasingly frequent. Protesters closed the city airport, considered a key point for their safety and to get their message to the international community and the police began trying to stop protesters throwing eggs, bricks and Molotov cocktails with tear gas, air shots (which wounded a man) and water cannons.

In a rather clever and technological way. Thousands in recent months have used protest "means" never seen before: among others, lasers and gas masks to avoid facial recognition; chat via Telegram to circumvent Chinese digital surveillance; manga-style posters circulated virally on the net; street skittles and water to put out the smoke.

What do people aim with the protests?

At first, the protests were focused on asking that this reform not to be carried out, but as time went by, the claims were mutated towards the reinforcement of its Basic Law, its constitution model.
The protesters made five main requests: to withdraw the bill that envisages extradition to China (which took place on 24 October); the resignation of the head of the Hong Kong executive, Carrie Lam; an investigation into the brutality expressed by the police during the protests; the release of those who have been arrested; more democratic freedoms.

What are the consequences of the protests?

Mainland China is Hong Kong's main trading partner, but the city is primarily an international economic and financial centre. According to the American Chamber of Commerce in Hong Kong, several companies have reported "serious consequences of the outage", including disruption of supply chains. Hong Kong companies, have also come under pressure and have been accused of sympathizing with the protesters.
The cancelled flights have represented damage to the companies operating in the financial hub. The airport contributes 5% of the city's GDP. Over 74 million passengers travelled to and from Hong Kong last year. The airport operates 1,100 passenger and freight flights every day and serves around 200 destinations worldwide.

Hong Kong now

Beijing leaders do not seem to have the slightest intention of accepting the activists' requests. Although Hong Kong is a special case in China, accepting the demands of protesters would mean not only taking the risk that someone unwelcome comes to power but would also create a dangerous precedent: the possibility of a democratic change in the rest of the country, which would thus endanger the regime's absolute authority.
Protesters ask to be heard and that the police account for what they consider brutality. So far 6,500 people have been arrested, only the first day of this year were more than 400 arrests. The underlying problem will remain latent.
