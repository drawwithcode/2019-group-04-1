“An oasis of stability”

Chile was just like an oasis, that seems to be the only Latin American country that shows a strong economy and stability, but as well as most oasis, it turned out to be a mirage.

What triggered the protests?

The crisis initiated when, by recommendation of a public transportation panel, the president Sebastián Piñera decided to increase the cost of the ticket of metro, reaching its maximum value to 830 pesos (approx. 1,17USD). Some experts affirmed that the rate hike is just the tip of the iceberg, because what is really behind all of it is the inequality that has been the center of the main debate of the latest days, insisting that Chilean social gap is disproportionate, even though Chile has the strongest economy of Latin American countries. It’s also a cumulative discontent, where the 30 pesos of a metro ticket has unleashed the uneasiness of 30 years of a failing democracy that nowadays follow the same constitution that left the dictatorship of Pinochet.

What do people aim with the protests?

People were originally protesting because, though there has been a growth of the middle class, it is actually a precarious middle class, where there are lower pensions, high levels of debt and salary doesn’t represent anything compared to the expenses. So it turns out to be a situation of daily uncertainty.
But, even though it was an argued petition, the government responded with such force, that people ended up remembering the days of the dictatorship where many people was unfairly killed because of it. After days of protest, President Piñera imposed a state of emergency and a curfew, sending over 10.000 soldiers to the streets with armed vehicles.

The state of emergency was lifted, and yet the police are still taking violent action against people. Since then 20 people have died, 1000 were injured and more than 3500 has been arrested. The public opinion is now pushing for the impeachment of Pinera if he doesn’t listen to people and a “constituent assembly” for a new constitution, along with their original requests.

What are the consequences of the protests?

First of all, one of the big consequences has been the deep distrust of the current political class and Pinera’s administration. Chile’s president failed constantly understanding people claims turning the protest into what he himself called publicly: “a war”. Since then the number of deaths, detentions, tortures and injured has rose up to thousands, and the government has been denouncing a systematic violation of people’s rights, whether they protested or not.
After a month, this turns out to be the most sustained protests Chile has seen since the end of military dictatorship, and this marks a precedent not only for Latin America, but to the world. People has managed to push the president to delivered his final concession: rewrite the country’s constitution.

Chile now

The general feeling right now could be called: “uncertainty”, because even if the new constitution gets rewritten, there’s no guarantee of Chile being again what it once was. What people are protesting for, is really a complaint built up over generations that finally are being told. But that’s also the big problem, those issues couldn’t be solve only with the promise of a new constitution, it needs most of all, time, something that President Pinera seems not to have. Hence, as many other countries, the Chilean protest is because of an historical widespread inequality, even though Chile was the one apparently safest country among a turbulent Latin America. This protest has shown to the world, how if this matters are not taking into account carefully, the same mistakes could be repeated elsewhere. Which is actually the irony, because is the rooted reason why some other countries are protesting as well.
